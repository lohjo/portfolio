Three questions frame this analysis. First: has my prior work actually been practice for what this role demands — not just adjacent to it, but genuinely rehearsal? Second: to what extent does the role accept that skills change — that a hire is a bet on trajectory, not a completed checklist? Third: how much does the role actually require unconventional thinking and building things at scale independently? The answers sort these companies into three tiers, and the write-ups are honest about where I am a strong fit, a stretch, or a mismatch.

EverComm
CleanTech · AI SG-based Series B
Strong Fit
🇸🇬 Singapore (NTU-incubated)

An NTU-born cleantech AI company that turns energy and emissions data into decisions — winner of the COP28 UAE Tech Sprint, S$10M Series B co-led by Mitsubishi Electric, and active across APAC, Taiwan, Europe, and with the UN.

Has my prior work been rehearsal for this?

More directly than almost any other company on this list. The CO₂ adsorption research you run every Friday at SUTD is not just thematically adjacent to EverComm's mission — it shares the same fundamental domain: carbon management, data-driven process analysis, and building tools to make sustainability decisions more precise. Fitting breakthrough curves, interpreting LDF mass-transfer coefficients, and presenting those findings to a policy audience at STEAMunity all map almost exactly onto what EverComm does: collect emissions data, model it rigorously, and turn the output into something actionable for enterprise and government clients. The Access fellowship energy management project — where your team built a three-month system to equip schools with best-in-class energy efficiency solutions, complete with a Gantt chart and business model — is a near-direct prototype of EverComm's B2B engagement model. When an interviewer asks "what do you know about carbon accounting?" you can answer with kinetics, not just keywords.

Does the role accept trajectory over completeness?

Yes. EverComm is a 50-person startup at Series B — a stage where every hire is expected to grow into a larger role within 12–18 months, not arrive fully formed. They partner with the UN and enterprise clients, which means interns and junior engineers are expected to eventually own problems, not just complete tasks. The "tick all checkboxes" candidate here would need a background in IoT, carbon accounting standards (ISO 14064-1), ML deployment, and enterprise sales — nobody at poly level has all of that. What matters is whether you can learn the carbon accounting framework fast, bring genuine domain depth in adsorption science, and operate with the cross-functional discipline you've demonstrated in Leo Club and SAIL.

Unconventional thinking + scale-building?

High on both. EverComm doesn't sell a generic SaaS; it builds site-specific models for complex industrial and commercial energy systems. The work requires people who can interrogate data in ways standard frameworks don't anticipate — which aligns with how you approached the CO₂ project (nine empirical breakthrough models, AICc comparison, Akaike weights). Their expansion into Europe and partnership with Mitsubishi Electric also means there are real-scale implementation problems to work on. Building autonomously is explicitly expected at a company this size.

Your SENTINEL work (deploying AI systems on Cloud Run, multi-agent orchestration) plus your genuine grasp of pedagogy from three different volunteer contexts makes you a rare combination: someone who can sit in an engineering sprint and a curriculum design session and contribute meaningfully to both.

A note from your interview feedback

Your mock session feedback flagged three things worth carrying into every one of these applications. First, lead with the implication, not the apparatus — don't open with "I built SENTINEL on Cloud Run," open with "I built a system to help multilingual communities assess information credibility in real time, and I deployed it to production." The apparatus comes second. Second, you say too many things — the feedback suggested keeping to three points that match the job description, not a portfolio dump. Third, say something they'll remember you by — not just skills, but the tension: "I've tutored lower-income kids in maths and I've built multi-agent AI systems. The reason I care about both is the same: the gap between what people are capable of and what their circumstances let them access." That's the sentence that makes a hiring manager say your name in the debrief.
